As with any catalog, the NASA-Sloan Atlas should be used with some caution. In general, for galaxies with suspicious or unusual catalog parameters, or which are very bright, it is worth using the NSAID to look them up in our search form to determine whether there are comments about them or obvious problems in the images. A good rule of thumb is that most NGC or UGC objects are fine, but most Messier objects are not.

More particularly:

  1. i and z band sky fluctuations: The sky subtraction method used leaves some low surface brightness galaxies sensitive to sky subtraction issues. About 5% of the sky area of SDSS has i and z band sky variations that our sky subtraction does not track well. This affects galaxies of low surface brightness (μ50>22.5), sometimes substantially affecting their red colors.
  2. Deblending issues: Although in general the data is better than the SDSS photometry, there are known to still be some deblending problems in the NSA. In particular, near bright stars and galaxies there are still errors. For the largest galaxies, particularly those brighter than r<12, deblending can substantially affect the flux.
  3. Distances: The distances and absolute magnitudes are based on the velocity model of Willick et al. 1997. In general, this can differ from other estimates, and in particular we have not included direct distance estimates for the nearest objects.
  4. GALEX fluxes: For the largest objects, the GALEX fluxes are underestimated by about 0.3 mag relative to Gil de Paz et al. (2009). The nature of that discrepancy is unclear.
  5. Matching to SDSS spectroscopy: The SDSS spectroscopy is used as a source catalog for redshifts in generating the NSA. Sometimes these spectra are "off-center" as determined in our photometric analysis. Therefore, we have separately matched the final measured centers to the SDSS spectroscopy. In cases selected from the SDSS catalog but for which there is no SDSS spectrum at the galaxy center, the redshifts listed are from the off-center spectrum, and we list no other spectroscopic measurements. If there is an SDSS spectrum at the galaxy center, the redshift from that spectrum is used, and we list the spectroscopic measurements from Yan et al. (2011). In this manner, except for redshifts, we have only listed spectroscopic measurements for galaxy centers, not for off-center spectra.

Caveats associated with previous version of the catalog are given below:

  1. Astrometric errors: In v0_1_2 and earlier, at declinations above 41 deg, there can be small astrometric errors in the mosaics and catalog positions, up to about a 0.25 arcsec shift. The nature of these errors are described in the SDSS DR8 documentation.
